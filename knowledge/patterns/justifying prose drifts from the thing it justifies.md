# justifying prose drifts from the thing it justifies

An instruction's stated rationale, illustrating example, or cited precedent turns out to be false while the
instruction itself stays sound. The drift is low-consequence right up until it isn't: **rationale is what a
later agent reasons from when a case is unclear**, and it travels further than the instruction it attends —
into generated artifacts, into propagated deltas, into other people's graphs.

Five instances: a test asserting a property its template did not have; a premise true on one path written
unconditionally across both; a tier definition falsified by item one of the same change; a decision citing
a precedent that does the opposite; an example whose case has a population of zero.

Four were caught before or shortly after shipping. One shipped and propagated, which is the instructive
one — correcting rationale after it has been stamped downstream requires superseding it, not editing it,
because the append-only contract protects what a graph has already recorded.

The mechanism is ordinary: once a decision is made, a supporting citation is reached for in the direction of
sounding supportive, and is not checked against the cited artifact. Reviews catch the instruction; the
rationale outlives the review.

*One witness (2026-07-22). Open.*
