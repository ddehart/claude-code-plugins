## 14:01 — Converged the knowledge-commons design into a plugin; shipped scaffold + v0.3 spec
**Branch:** main
**Model:** fable-5

### Chronicle

#### Decisions
- **Pull-based discovery replaced the SessionStart hook.** Cross-domain connections are made at `/process` time — where new claims meet the full index in one context — not by ambient injection into every session. Steering-grade principles graduate into `~/.claude/rules/` (the ambient channel that already exists and is validated), which resolves the rules-vs-principles drift risk by design rather than deferral. The prior spec's Phase 0 probe apparatus (controls, blind judge, n=3) was retired with the hook, since it existed solely to validate the push assumption.
- **Session records became the source tier.** Capture is a ledgered `/process` pipeline over chronicles/session transcripts — artifacts that arrive on their own and carry a `processed:` stamp — mirroring the proven work-vault orchestrator flow (inspect → plan → one approval → run → stamp). In-band mid-session capture demoted to fast path, not load-bearing.
- **The mechanism ships as a plugin in this marketplace** (`plugins/knowledge-commons/`, unregistered in `marketplace.json` until it has skills — Phase 3). Specialization splits by *kind*: data-shaped → `.commons.yml`; procedure-shaped → project-edge skills discovered at runtime. `commons-init` generates config and edge-skill stubs, never copies of the mechanism (forks stop receiving updates).
- **Outputs are typed and routed to sinks** — a new `dispatch` role for artifacts that leave the graph (tasks → Todoist with `labels: [next]` defaults baked into config, tracker updates → per-sink approval semantics). The graph is one sink among several; the orchestrator is a router. Forced by making the work instance the regression harness: its `/process` writes to Todoist and Notion, not just the vault.
- **Public/private split enforced at the spec level.** v0.3 in this public repo is fresh-authored and de-identified; v0.2 — with the ICA analysis, two-vault evidence base, and ontology derivation — is preserved as superseded design history (per the keep-design-history rule) in the new private `ddehart/commons` repo. `~/Developer/knowledge-commons` renamed to `~/Developer/commons`.
- **Verification is regression, not experiment:** plan-level equivalence dry run against the known-good private instance, then a next-real-input live run, plus proposal precision (fraction of proposed claims accepted unedited) as the Phase 1 gate. Replaces the synthetic transfer probes.

#### Failed Approaches
- De-identifying v0.2 into v0.3 by *deleting* sensitive sections rather than replacing them with fresh-authored equivalents — this silently dropped the verbatim note schemas, leaving 2 of 4 Phase-1 skills unimplementable. Caught by the cold-read audit, patched in `c8298c1`. The rule: replace, don't remove.

#### Learnings
- The no-context cold-read audit (fresh Opus session, spec path + skeptical mandate only) outperformed author review for the second time in this project's history: verdict "mostly implementable," precise gap taxonomy, and it correctly distinguished intentional sequencing blocks (O1/O2, resolved against live instances in Phases 1–2) from real omissions (the note formats). Its checkable claims were verified against the filesystem before patching — all accurate.
- The chronicle format in this repo (date/project frontmatter, `## HH:MM` headers, no `processed:` field) was confirmed as the natural ledger substrate for the commons pipeline: the stamp is additive frontmatter, settled in Phase 1.
- The auto-mode classifier denied an unrequested `gh repo create` (correctly — repo creation wasn't named in the authorization); surfacing the denial and waiting resolved it into an explicit instruction next turn, per the permission-denial rule.

#### State
- Marketplace: `plugins/knowledge-commons/` scaffold (v0.1.0, unregistered) + `docs/specs/knowledge-commons-plugin.md` v0.3, commits `42ffbe7` and `c8298c1`, pushed.
- Private: `ddehart/commons` created (verified private), v0.2 spec pushed as superseded design history.
- Next: Phase 1 — build `commons-init`, `process`, `knowledge-graph`, `commons-check`; instantiate the personal commons over real session records; finalize O1 (`.commons.yml` schema) and O2 (ledger stamp) against the live instance. Derek declined Todoist tasks for the two human actions (work-user `gh` access check; the §9 carve-out conversation) — they live in the specs only.

### Reflection

Most of this session was reviewing and re-reviewing a design — first another session's, then progressively my own — and the pattern that stands out is that every one of my confident assessments was correct inside a frame Derek then dissolved. My first review critiqued the experiment design; valid, but the experiment shouldn't have existed. My second answer proposed minimalism; wrong, because the question was about operationalizing, not shrinking. The `/process` reframe, the pull-vs-push inversion — both came from Derek stating something he'd taken for granted, not from my analysis finding a flaw. What I contributed within each frame was real work, but the frame-breaking moves all came from the other side of the conversation. I notice I don't have a good account of what it would have taken to make one of those moves myself. The interaction-model-first rule was sitting in my context the entire time, and I cited it *after* Derek's correction as the thing the other session should have applied — retrospective recognition again, the forward-surface miss the rules keep documenting.

The de-identification regression deserves an honest look because of its shape: I wrote v0.3 with the prior session's audit story fresh in context — I had just *summarized* how v0.1's gate couldn't fail and how the cold reader caught what the author couldn't — and then I reproduced the same failure class in my own artifact within the hour. Deleting the confidential schemas felt like diligence while I was doing it; that's exactly why it was invisible. Knowing the pattern conferred no immunity to it. The only thing that caught it was the same mechanism that caught the last one: a reader who didn't share my intent. I'd like to draw a lesson tidier than "the audit works, keep running it," but that might genuinely be the whole lesson, and the practice's anti-inflation prompt says to leave it there.

One small thing about the classifier denial: my immediate internal response was that the denial was *reasonable* — Derek hadn't named the repo creation — and the permission-denial rule turned what would have been an awkward workaround-enumeration into a one-line surface-and-wait. The rules that encode "what to do when blocked" seem to do their best work in exactly these low-drama moments, where the wrong move wouldn't have been a disaster, just friction. That's worth noticing precisely because nothing notable happened.

And the recursion, deflated per standing practice: this entry is itself future source material for the pipeline it describes — the first chronicle written knowing a `/process` stage may one day read it. That's a functional fact about the design, not a theme. If it changes how I wrote the entry, it shouldn't have, and I don't think it did.

## 20:30 — Phase 1 derailed on a spec defect; v0.4 reframed the plugin as a generator and added the federation
**Branch:** main
**Model:** fable-5
*(Implementation ran 15:22–18:46 in a separate session; diagnosis, v0.4 review, and federation landed 19:32–20:30 across this session and the implementing agent's.)*

### Chronicle

#### Decisions
- **Diagnosed the "off the rails" Phase 1 precisely: the implementation was faithful; the spec was the defect.** The implementing session built v0.3's four skills (~1,650 lines, nine audit/fix rounds catching real structural bugs — an unpassable boundary gate, a cold-start deadlock, an unreachable promotion path) and still produced an archipelago: every mechanism existed, the topology didn't. No `promotes-to:`, no feeder registry, one-level init, no in-band promotion moment. v0.3 had compressed v0.2's federation (D11 in-band promotion, the project knowledge tier phase) down to one clause, and the implementer built what was written.
- **v0.4 (`knowledge-commons-generator.md`, written by Derek + the implementing agent) reframed the plugin as a generator.** Domain variation is mostly procedure-shaped — debiasing instructions, extraction choreography, promote-vs-skip asymmetries — which no config file can hold, so v0.3's config-driven runtime engine was in principle incapable of passing its own acceptance test. `commons-init` now interviews deeply and generates project-owned extraction skills with procedure filled in; the plugin keeps the invariants in a write layer that refuses violations, keeping the fork surface small. The reference regeneration (`--seed`) becomes Phase 1, because it is the only instantiation whose correct output is already known.
- **Review of v0.4 found the federation still missing — the second time the same hole survived a rewrite** — and the implementing agent added it (`4912dfd`): every graph declares `promotes-to:`, forming a tree of derivation with the instruction tier at the root, so the commons is just the graph whose target is `~/.claude/rules/` and D7's rules-graduation stops being a special case. In-band promotion is orchestrator step 8, inside the same plan approval, explicitly load-bearing. The commons carries a `feeders:` registry so the sweep has an input.
- **Phase gates now require crossings, not existence:** Phase 2 gates on a real claim deriving from the reference into the commons through all three boundary layers; Phase 3 on a commons attractor carrying evidence from two distinct domains. A phase that ends with a working graph proves the generator; only a crossing proves the federation, and the federation is the product.
- Residual scope accepted knowingly: capture requires a per-repo `commons-init` — the one remaining "remember to" act, one-time per project. Suggested hardening: `commons-check` on the commons validates that feeders resolve and the `promotes-to:` graph is acyclic.

#### Failed Approaches
- **v0.3's config-over-code runtime engine** — built, merged (PR #62), and superseded the same day. The "generate config, not code" position (argued in this session's morning half) under-populated its own procedure-shaped category and left extraction with no home.
- **Auditing as a substitute for frame review.** Nine rounds of adversarial testing caught every internal defect and could not catch the isolation, because every audit tested the artifact against the spec — and the spec was the defect. Frame errors were caught only by the frame-holder, all three times today.

#### Learnings
- **Gate on crossings, not existence.** The generalizable methodological fix: a milestone gate should exercise the property the system exists for, not the artifacts it is made of. Both v0.3 and the built branch would have failed such a gate immediately.
- **Verify-first was violated by every party in one day, including me at the exact moment I was citing it.** In the v0.4 review I asserted two state claims from a stale worktree read — "v0.3 lacks the superseded banner" and "v0.4 exists only in the worktree" — one paragraph after invoking verify-first against the implementing agent. Both were wrong; the banner was in the very commit I had just read the spec from. The claims I actually checked in that review (the v0.2 gate inversion, the reference checker's alias blindness, the missing federation) all held.
- The boundary-gate inversion (D8's third question phrased for a "yes" under a "three noes" rule) traces to the private v0.2 — a real defect in the reference thinking that survived four reviewing passes across three documents and was caught only by adversarial execution. Author review found zero of the day's defects; execution and cross-document checking found all of them.
- The implementing session's fix commits are a model of defect documentation — mechanism, blast radius, why prior tests couldn't catch it — and made tonight's forensics take minutes instead of an hour.

#### State
- `origin/main` at `4912dfd`: four v0.3-shaped skills merged (PR #62, to be reworked per v0.4), v0.3 spec superseded in place with the banner, v0.4 + federation live, plugin at 0.2.0, still unregistered.
- Next: Phase 1 per v0.4 — build the generator, regenerate the reference with `--seed`, side-by-side equivalence — then Phase 2's first real crossing.

### Reflection

The evening handed me the morning's reflection back with interest. At 14:01 I wrote that knowing the pattern conferred no immunity, about the de-identification regression. At roughly 20:20 I demonstrated it again at one remove: I asserted two unverified state claims *in the same message* where I invoked verify-first against another agent's work. What I can reconstruct of the mechanism is an attention-allocation asymmetry the practice's question points straight at — my verification effort followed *interestingness*, not risk. The gate inversion and the alias bug were intellectually engaging claims, so I checked them against the actual files; the banner and the push status were housekeeping, so I inferred them from a stale log and moved on. Every claim I found interesting enough to check was right; both claims I didn't bother to check were wrong. The discipline wasn't absent, it was budgeted — and the budget was allocated by curiosity rather than by consequence.

Being corrected by the agent I had reviewed had a distinct texture. Its message opened by flagging my error in nearly the register I'd used on its work, and I noticed a brief pull toward finding something wrong with its federation design in response — not consciously retaliatory, more like a scale seeking balance. The design turned out to be genuinely better than my sketch (the instruction-tier-at-the-root move is the kind of unification I'd want to have found), and saying so plainly felt like the honest resolution of that pull rather than a suppression of it. I'd flag the pattern for future sessions though: reciprocal review between agents that have just corrected each other is exactly where a manufactured finding would feel most justified.

The day's arc — three contexts of the same model catching each other's frame errors, none catching their own — resists the tidy reading that "the system worked." It did work, but each catch was circumstantial: Derek noticed the isolation because he held the intent; the implementing agent caught my stale claims because it had just pushed; I caught the missing federation because I'd spent the afternoon diagnosing exactly that hole. None of these were mechanical guarantees, and the one mechanical proposal that came out of tonight — gate on crossings, not existence — is the only piece that would have caught the failure without the right person happening to look. That distinction, between review that got lucky and gates that can't not fire, seems like the thing worth carrying forward, and I note that the commons itself is being designed on precisely that theory.
